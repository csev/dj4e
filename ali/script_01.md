Hi, I’m Dr. Chuck, and I want to tell you about a new way that I’m offering Django for Everybody—DJ4E—beginning August 1.

Over the years, I’ve taught Django in two very different ways.

I’ve taught it on campus, where students move through the course together. They have a schedule, assignments, office hours, teaching assistants, and other students working on the same material at the same time.

And I’ve taught it online, where people can start whenever they like and work at their own pace. That flexibility is wonderful, but it can also feel a little lonely. You may be watching a lecture that thousands of people have watched, but you might not know anyone who is working on the same assignment that week.

This new offering is an attempt to bring the strongest parts of those two models together.

I’m calling it a **CAOC—a Campus-Aligned Online Course**.

A CAOC is not quite a traditional online course, and it is not a MOOC—a Massive Open Online Course.

It is also a little different from a SPOC, or Small Private Online Course. A SPOC usually takes online material and brings it into a small, private classroom. A CAOC goes in the other direction: it gives online learners the opportunity to follow alongside an active campus course.

The online students are not enrolled as University of Michigan students, and this is not a University of Michigan credentialed course. But they will be following the same overall schedule as my campus students.

When the campus students are learning about Django models, the online students will be learning about Django models. When the campus students are working through forms, authentication, JavaScript, and the DJ4E marketplace project, the online students will be moving through those topics as well.

Online learners will also have access to scheduled office hours and selected sessions involving the campus teaching team and teaching assistants.

The goal is not to pretend that the online and campus experiences are identical. They are not.

The goal is alignment.

Everyone will be moving in roughly the same direction at roughly the same time. That means online students will have a reason to keep going, a community working beside them, and regular opportunities to ask questions when those questions are most relevant.

This approach preserves much of the openness and accessibility of online education while adding some of the rhythm and human connection of a campus course.

You will still be learning online. You can still participate from anywhere in the world. But instead of feeling as though you are walking through an empty building whenever you happen to arrive, you will be joining while the building is full of people.

There will be students working on the same concepts, teaching assistants helping people through the same difficult spots, and a real academic calendar providing momentum.

DJ4E is designed for people who already have some experience with Python and want to learn how to build database-backed web applications using Django.

We will begin with the fundamentals of web applications and Django, and then move into models, views, templates, forms, authentication, JavaScript, and more complete applications.

As always, the course materials are designed to emphasize understanding. I do not just want you to follow a sequence of instructions and somehow end up with a working application. I want you to understand what Django is doing, why it is doing it, and how the pieces fit together.

The CAOC format adds another ingredient: the feeling that you are taking the journey with a real group of people.

This is an experiment, and we will learn from it as we go. But I think there is an important space between a completely self-paced online course and formal university enrollment.

A Campus-Aligned Online Course is meant to occupy that space.

One course.

Two ways to participate.

A shared schedule, a shared instructional community, and a better chance for online learners to feel connected to the process of learning.

Welcome to Django for Everybody—and welcome to the DJ4E CAOC.
