Hi, I’m Dr. Chuck, and I want to tell you about a new way that I’m offering Building Web Applications in Django, beginning August 1.

Over the years, I’ve taught Django in two very different ways.

I’ve taught it on campus, where students move through the course together. They have a schedule, assignments, office hours, teaching assistants, and other students working on the same material at the same time.

And I’ve taught it online, where people can often start whenever they like and work at their own pace. That flexibility is wonderful, but it can also feel a little lonely. You may be watching a lecture that thousands of people have watched, but you might not know anyone who is working on the same assignment that week.

This new offering is an attempt to bring the strongest parts of those two models together.

I’m calling it a CAOC—a Campus-Aligned Online Course.

Now, how do you pronounce CAOC?

I pronounce it “kay-ock.”

It sounds a little like kayak, which is actually a useful image. A two-person kayak works best when both people are moving in the same direction, at roughly the same pace.

It also sounds a little like chaos, which may be an equally accurate description of teaching campus and online students at the same time.

But the important word in CAOC is aligned.

A CAOC is an online course that is deliberately aligned with live campus courses.

In this case, the online course will run alongside SI 357 and SI 664, the two University of Michigan courses in which I teach this material.

The online students are not enrolled as University of Michigan students, and this is not a University of Michigan credentialed course. But they will follow the same overall schedule as the campus students.

When the students in SI 357 and SI 664 are learning about Django models, the online students will be learning about Django models.

When the campus students are working through forms, authentication, JavaScript, and complete Django applications, the online students will be moving through those topics as well.

Online learners will also have access to scheduled office hours and selected sessions involving the campus teaching team and teaching assistants.

The goal is not to pretend that the online and campus experiences are identical. They are not.

The goal is alignment.

Everyone will be moving in roughly the same direction at roughly the same time.

That means online students will have a reason to keep going, a community working beside them, and regular opportunities to ask questions when those questions are most relevant.

This approach preserves much of the accessibility of online education while adding some of the rhythm and human connection of a campus course.

You will still be learning online, and you can participate from anywhere in the world.

But instead of feeling as though you are walking through an empty building whenever you happen to arrive, you will be joining while the building is full of people.

There will be students working on the same concepts, teaching assistants helping people through the same difficult spots, and a real academic calendar providing momentum.

Building Web Applications in Django is designed for people who already have some experience with Python and want to learn how to build database-backed web applications using Django.

We will begin with the fundamentals of web applications and Django, and then move into models, views, templates, forms, authentication, JavaScript, and more complete applications.

As always, the course materials are designed to emphasize understanding.

I do not just want you to follow a sequence of instructions and somehow end up with a working application.

I want you to understand what Django is doing, why it is doing it, and how the pieces fit together.

The CAOC format adds another ingredient: the feeling that you are taking the journey with a real group of people.

Our first offering begins August 1.

The cost will be $300. That fee is intended primarily to cover the cost of the teaching assistants who will support the online students through office hours, instructional sessions, and help with the course material.

This is an experiment, and we will learn from it as we go.

Part of the experiment is finding the best ways for campus students and online students to learn alongside one another without pretending that their experiences are exactly the same.

I expect the course to evolve during the semester based on what we hear from the people taking it.

Online students will be encouraged to share feedback during office hours, in live sessions, and by email. Tell us what is working, what feels confusing, where you need more support, and which parts of the campus-aligned experience are most valuable.

The teaching assistants and I will pay attention not only to the technical questions students ask, but also to what those questions tell us about the design of the course.

When several people struggle with the same assignment, explanation, or piece of software, that is useful information.

We may add an example, clarify some instructions, adjust an office-hour session, or create additional material to address the problem.

We also want to learn what kinds of interaction are most useful to online students.

It might be traditional office hours, focused review sessions, demonstrations, question-and-answer meetings, or simply knowing that there are other students working through the same material at the same time.

So the students in this first offering will not just be taking a course.

They will also be helping us understand how a Campus-Aligned Online Course should work.

The goal is to listen, make thoughtful adjustments during the course when we can, and use what we learn to improve future offerings.

I think there is an important space between a completely self-paced online course and formal university enrollment.

A Campus-Aligned Online Course is meant to occupy that space.

One course.

Campus and online learners.

A shared schedule, a shared instructional community, and a better chance for online learners to feel connected to the process of learning.

So welcome to Building Web Applications in Django.

Welcome to the experiment.

And welcome to my first "Campus Aligned Online Course".  Pronounced “kay-ock,” somewhere between a kayak and chaos.
